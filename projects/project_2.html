MedX community
soundings wall

Sounding board installation made in collaboration with attendees at a non-traditional medical conference. Goal: to prompt the community to surface needs, share resources, expertise, and experiences in organic and delightful ways.

Role:

Experience design, early concept, installation

Year:

2016

Tags:

#community, #soundingboard, #crowdsourced, #interactive

Learn More

Medicine X is a conference with a mission:
To recognize all stakeholders for their expertise in health care, and give traditionally underheard voices—patients and caregivers—the opportunity to lead the conversation about the future of health care.

Problem one

Academic medical conferences are known for being exclusive to medical professionals and industry leaders. One-third of the Med X audience is patient or caregiver. They are leaders and experts - but at an academic medical conference, they may not feel that way.


How might we place inclusivity at the center of the conference experience?

Problem two

The conference encourages participants to look for their “next big idea” or next collaborators in unexpected places. But it can be difficult to find those people amidst the hectic schedule and numerous activities and sessions in parallel.


How might we lessen the feeling of “missing out” and increase the number of “aha!” connections?

Problem three

With increasingly more connections being made virtually, transient physical gatherings like conferences lend a special urgency to everyone participating. For organizers, the the days of the conference are themselves stories, with unique highlights and milestones.


How might we foster the story of a conference, marked through connections made and other noteworthy moments?

Solution

Community prompt card-based soundings wall that encourages interactive discussion, sharing and connection. Each card contains one of three open-ended prompts that attendees could fill out.

The sounding board was an open invitation to all.

Attendees were given stickers with their twitter handles to add to their own cards or to others’ cards. Viewers could see others interested in similar topics, and could connect directly via social media.

Attendees could “upload” their contributions to the community wall and view others’ posts across all three days of the conference.

The broad range of responses and requests for help reflected the immense diversity of the Medicine X audience.

The installation remained across three days of the conference for public contribution and viewing.

The installation surfaced expertise and interest in under-represented topics in academic medicine that was helpful to the conference organizers. Transgender health was a topic at the subsequent conference in April 2017.

Cardwriters did not take the exercise too seriously. Which we loved! Others used the cards as a way to invite others to connect after conference hours for meals and drinks.

As we hoped, many card writers used the offer for help as a launchpad for new collaborations or to inform others of their existing communities.

Over the course of three days the soundings wall received over 230 contributions.

Observation one

Did the project provide an alternative means of engagement for attendees? Yes. We found that our experiment—aimed at preserve the sense of spontaneity and discovery in the in-person human gathering —was well subscribed, even for our incredibly plugged-in and social-media-savvy attendees.



The installation garnered over 230 contributions across three days of the conference.

Observation two

What was the impact of the wall? Hard to say. We may not be able to measure all the new collaborations, projects or ideas our soundings wall sparked. However, we’re excited to see members of the Med X taking the concept of participatory physical discussion into their own communities.

Observation three

For a further iteration, I would love to take the collected cards and digitize them and make them searchable online. It might be an interesting way to give the soundings wall a life after the conference.
